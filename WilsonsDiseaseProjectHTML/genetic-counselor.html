Genetic Counselor's Report: Wilson's Disease

Updated 2024

Inheritance Pattern

Wilson's disease follows what we call an autosomal recessive pattern. In plain English, this means:

  • Inheritance pattern: You need two copies of the faulty gene (one from each parent) to develop the disease
  • Passing it on: When both parents are carriers, there's a 1 in 4 chance their child will have Wilson's
  • Carrier status: If one parent has Wilson's, their children will all be carriers, but won't develop the disease unless the other parent is also a carrier
  • Gene location: The culprit is the ATP7B gene on chromosome 13
  • Testing options: We can test for both carrier status and diagnosis

Understanding this inheritance pattern helps families make informed decisions about future pregnancies and allows relatives to know if they should be tested too.

Punnett Square

Here's a simple way to visualize how Wilson's disease can be passed down:

W (normal) w (Wilson's)
W (normal) WW (normal) Ww (carrier)
w (Wilson's) Ww (carrier) ww (affected)

Looking at this square, when both parents are carriers (they have one normal gene "W" and one Wilson's gene "w"):

  • There's a 1 in 4 (25%) chance their child will have Wilson's disease
  • There's a 2 in 4 (50%) chance their child will be a carrier like them
  • There's a 1 in 4 (25%) chance their child will inherit two normal genes

Looking at this family tree, we can see several patterns typical of Wilson's disease:

  • Carrier parents can have a mix of affected, carrier, and unaffected children
  • Affected individuals only appear when they get two copies of the faulty gene
  • Carriers can pass the gene through generations without anyone showing symptoms
  • The disease can skip generations showing up in grandchildren but not in children
  • Both boys and girls can be affected equally

These patterns help doctors and genetic counselors identify who might be at risk in a family with Wilson's disease history.

Genetic Testing

If you're considering genetic testing for Wilson's disease, here's what's available:

  • Gene sequencing: Looks for specific changes in your ATP7B gene
  • Carrier testing: For relatives of someone with Wilson's – especially siblings who have a 1 in 2 chance of being carriers
  • Prenatal testing: Options for couples who know they're both carriers
  • Newborn screening: Not routinely done yet, but some specialists advocate for it
  • Diagnostic testing: For people showing possible symptoms of Wilson's

I always recommend talking with a genetic counselor before and after testing they can explain what the results actually mean for you and your family.

Family Planning Considerations

If Wilson's disease runs in your family, here are some options to consider when planning for children:

  • Know your chances: Understanding the odds helps you make informed choices
  • PGD (embryo testing): Some couples use IVF with embryo testing to select unaffected embryos
  • During pregnancy testing: Options like CVS or amniocentesis if you want to know during pregnancy
  • Testing the extended family: Encouraging relatives to get tested can help identify carriers
  • Talking it through: A genetic counselor can help you work through these complex decisions

Remember, there's no right or wrong choice here just what works best for your situation and values.
