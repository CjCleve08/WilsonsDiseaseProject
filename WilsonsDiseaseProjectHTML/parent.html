Parent's Guide to Wilson's Disease

Updated 2024

What You Need to Know

Finding out your kid has Wilson's disease can be really scary. This guide will help you understand what's going on, how to work with doctors, and how to help your kid and your family.

Wilson's disease is rare it's when your body can't handle copper right. But don't worry too much if your kid takes their medicine and follows the doctor's advice, they can still live a pretty normal life.

Taking Care of Your Kid Every Day

You'll need to make some changes to help your kid stay healthy:

  • Medicine time: Make sure they take their penicillamine or zinc every day
  • Food stuff: Keep them away from foods with lots of copper
  • Doctor visits: Take them to regular check-ups
  • Exercise: Help them stay active in ways that work for them
  • Sleep: Make sure they get enough rest

These changes will help your kid feel better and stay healthy.

Helping Your Kid Feel Better

Having Wilson's disease can be tough on kids and families:

  • Feeling down: Your kid might feel sad or worried sometimes
  • Family stuff: Things might feel different at home
  • Self-esteem: Help your kid feel good about themselves
  • Future worries: Your kid might worry about what's ahead
  • Making friends: Help them connect with other kids

Talking to a counselor or joining a support group can help everyone feel better.

Help at School

Your kid might need some extra help at school:

  • More time for tests: Since they might get tired easily or have side effects from medicine
  • Help with notes: If their hands shake or they have trouble writing
  • Understanding about missing school: For doctor appointments and treatments
  • Technology help: Like programs that turn speech into text
  • Working with the school nurse: To make sure your kid gets their medicine at school

Talk to your kid's teachers and school counselor about what they need. Most schools have plans they can put in place to help kids with medical needs.

Places to Get Help

You don't have to figure everything out by yourself. Here are some places that can help:

  • Support groups: Meeting other families dealing with Wilson's disease
  • Money help: Programs that might help pay for medicine and doctor visits
  • Learning more: Books and websites about Wilson's disease
  • Special doctors: Finding doctors who know a lot about Wilson's disease
  • Help at home: Services that might help with day-to-day stuff

Don't be afraid to reach out. There are lots of people who want to help you just need to ask.

Talking About It

Talking about Wilson's disease with your kid, family, and others is really important:

  • Talking to your kid: Be honest but positive let them know they can still do lots of cool stuff
  • Talking to siblings: Help brothers and sisters understand what's going on
  • Telling other people: Help your kid decide who to tell and how much to share
  • Talking to doctors: Learn how to explain what's happening and ask good questions
  • Talking to teachers: Make sure they know what your kid needs

Being open and honest (in a way that makes sense for your kid's age) usually works best. Kids can handle more than we sometimes think.

Looking Ahead

As your kid grows up, you'll face new challenges and opportunities:

  • College: Finding schools with good support for medical needs
  • Jobs: Thinking about careers that work well with Wilson's disease
  • Dating: Helping your kid feel confident and know when to share about their condition
  • Taking over their care: Teaching your kid to manage their own medicine and doctor visits
  • Having kids someday: Understanding the genetic side of Wilson's disease

With the right support, kids with Wilson's disease can go to college, have great careers, and build wonderful families of their own.
