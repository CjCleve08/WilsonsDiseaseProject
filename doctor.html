Doctor's Medical Report: Wilson's Disease

Updated 2024

Executive Summary

Wilson's disease is a rare genetic disorder that causes too much copper to build up in your body. It affects about 1 in 30,000 people worldwide. The disease happens because of a problem with the ATP7B protein.

The disease was first described by Dr. Samuel Alexander Kinnier Wilson in 1912, who found the brain problems and changes. Without proper treatment, Wilson's disease can cause serious liver damage, brain problems, and even death.

What Happens When You Have Wilson's Disease

Wilson's disease can affect your body in different ways. The main problems usually happen in your liver and brain:

  • Liver problems: Your skin might turn yellow, you feel tired a lot, your belly hurts, and your liver doesn't work right
  • Brain problems: Trouble with movement, shaking hands, stiff muscles, trouble talking, changes in behavior, memory problems
  • Brain problems: Your hands might shake, your muscles get stiff, it's hard to talk clearly, and sometimes you feel down or anxious
  • Eye problems: Copper builds up in your eyes, making rings around your iris
  • Heart problems: Sometimes your heart beats weird or gets weak
  • Bone problems: Your bones might get weak and your joints might hurt

Different people have different problems. Some people's livers get sick first, while others have brain problems first. This makes it tricky for doctors to figure out what's wrong.

What's Going On Inside Your Body

Wilson's disease happens because of a problem in your genes (like a typo in your body's instructions). This messes up how your body handles copper. Your body needs a little copper to work right, but too much is bad news. In Wilson's disease, your body can't get rid of extra copper, so it builds up in your liver first, then your brain and other parts of your body.

Think of it like a garbage truck that's broken - it can't pick up the trash (copper) and take it away. The trash keeps piling up in your body until it starts causing problems.

How Doctors Find Out If You Have It

Doctors use a bunch of tests to figure out if someone has Wilson's disease:

  • Blood test: Checks if you have enough of a protein that helps handle copper
  • Pee test: Measures how much copper is in your urine
  • Liver check: Takes a tiny piece of your liver to look at under a microscope
  • Gene test: Looks for the problem in your genes
  • Eye exam: Checks for copper rings in your eyes

It's super important to find out early if someone has Wilson's disease. The sooner doctors know, the better they can help prevent permanent damage to your body.

How Doctors Help You Get Better

Doctors help people with Wilson's disease in a few ways:

  • Medicine: Pills that help your body get rid of extra copper
  • Zinc: Helps stop your body from taking in too much copper
  • Food changes: Stay away from foods with lots of copper
  • Regular check ups: Doctors keep an eye on your copper levels and how your body is doing
  • Liver transplant: Sometimes doctors need to give you a new liver if yours is too sick

You'll need to keep taking medicine and following the doctor's advice for the rest of your life. If you stop, the disease can get worse really fast. But if you stick with the treatment, most people can live pretty normal lives.

What to Expect

How well someone does with Wilson's disease depends on a few things:

  • Finding it early: The sooner doctors know, the better
  • Taking medicine: You've got to keep taking your pills
  • Liver health: How much damage your liver has when doctors find it
  • Brain problems: How bad your brain symptoms are
  • Seeing the doctor: Going to regular check ups

If doctors find it early and you take your medicine, you can live a pretty normal life. But if you don't get treatment, Wilson's disease can be really dangerous usually because of liver problems or brain problems.

New Stuff Doctors Are Working On

Doctors and scientists are trying to find better ways to help people with Wilson's disease:

  • Gene fixes: Trying to fix the problem in your genes
  • New medicines: Looking for better pills with fewer side effects
  • Better tests: Making it easier to find Wilson's disease early
  • Brain protection: Finding ways to keep your brain healthy
  • Liver help: Working on ways to fix liver damage

Doctors haven't found a complete cure yet, but they're working on making treatments better and helping people live better lives with Wilson's disease.
